Learning Objectives

In this lesson, students will learn how to use the following SketchUp tools:

Select

Rotate

Move

Orbit

Zoom

Students will also practice the following workflows:

Grouping Geometry

Creating Scenes

Printing to Scale

At the completion of this lesson, students should feel comfortable with the following on their own:

Identifying joint locations in a model, and grouping geometry for separation along those joints. Manipulating groups of geometry into print ready sections.

Creating and saving different model views as scenes, and using those scenes as templates to print a scaled version of a model.

Assembling a 3D model using a 2D printed template by cutting, folding and gluing edges according to the template.

Intro to SketchUp for Schools

Before we get started, let’s go through some of the basics together.

Getting Access

  • Go to https://edu.sketchup.com/app
  • Sign in with the Google or Microsoft email address provided by your school.
  • Note: If you have trouble logging in, check with your administrator that your school or district has installed SketchUp for Schools (Instructions for Google & Microsoft Admins)

TIP

Save often!

If you get into the habit of saving your work, you’ll be less likely to lose any progress if class ends and you close your laptop.

Creating a New File

  • This is the SketchUp for Schools home screen. Here you can start a new project by clicking “Start Modeling” or “Create New”. Both will open SketchUp’s default template.

Choosing a Template

  • Or you can choose a template in your preferred unit of measurement. For this lesson, we’ll be using Architectural - Feet & Inches. Choosing a template will start a new project as well.

Saving Files

  • Whenever you start a new model, it’s a good idea to save your file first. Click on the menu icon on the top left of the screen, then click “Save As.”
  • Create a new folder by clicking on the New Folder Icon and give it a name.
  • Give your model a name and then press “OK.”
  • If you’ve done everything correctly, you’ll see your file name in the top left corner and a “Saved” message.

The Scale Figure

  • Every time you open a new model in SketchUp for Schools, you will see Katherine Johnson’s scale figure. Katherine’s job is to give us a sense of the size of the objects we draw in our model.
  • For example, Katherine is 5’5”. If we draw a 3-foot cube next to her, the cube will be about half her height.

Drawing a Cube

  • Select the Rectangle tool from the menu on the left.
  • Click once on the ground near Katherine’s feet to set one corner of your cube.
  • Without clicking again, move your mouse on the screen, type 3’, 3’ and press Enter.
  • Select the Push/Pull tool from the menu on the left.
  • Click once on the face you just drew. Without clicking again, move up to make a cube 3D.
  • Type 3’ and press Enter to complete your cube.

TIP

Unless otherwise specified, a click in SketchUp is executed as “click and release.”

Navigation Tools

  • One of the most important things to learn in 3D modeling is how to move around in your model window.
  • Click the orbit tool from the menu on the left to expand all the navigation tools.

Orbit

The Orbit tool allows you to rotate around your model.

Click on the Orbit tool, then left click-hold-drag your mouse from side to side in the model window.

Mouse shortcut: hold down the scroll wheel to activate the Orbit tool, then move your mouse in any direction to orbit.

Pan

The Pan tool allows you to move your model across your screen.

Click on the Pan tool, then left click-hold-drag your mouse from side to side in the model window.

Mouse shortcut: hold down the scroll wheel, then hold down the shift key at the same time. Move your mouse in any direction to pan.

Zoom

The Zoom tool allows you to look closer at the details in your model.

Click on the Zoom tool, then left click-hold-drag your mouse up and down in the model window.

Mouse shortcut: use the scroll wheel to zoom in and out.

Zoom Window

The Zoom Window tool allows you to select an area of your model to view closer. Click on the Zoom Window tool, then left click-hold-drag your mouse to highlight an area of your model.

Zoom Extents

The Zoom Extents tool allows you to see all the geometry in your model.

Click on the Zoom Extents tool and everything in your model will come into view.

The Instructor Panel

If you want to see how a tool works, use the Instructor Panel.

  • You can open the Instructor Panel from the right side menu to find helpful tips on how to use any of SketchUp’s tools.
  • The way it works: click on a tool with the Instructor panel open and you will see a description of the tool and a step-by-step guide on how to use it.

Search for a tool

  • All of the drawing tools can be found in the toolset along the left side of the screen. However, you can always use the Search function to find a desired tool more quickly. This search function is also where you will find all of SketchUp’s default keyboard shortcuts, and where you can edit them or add your own.

That’s it for the intro.

You’re ready to get started on modeling!

Unit of measurement.

This lesson is drawn using the Imperial System for measurement. This is common practice in the United States and differs from the Metric System. Whereas the metric system uses meters (m) and millimeters (mm), the Imperial System uses feet (ft) and inches (in). Feet may also be represented by the ( ‘ ) symbol and inches by ( “ ). 1 meter = 3.28084 ft

This lesson template uses inches as the default unit, meaning that all manually typed units will be entered as inches unless otherwise indicated. When manually entering other units, use a ( ‘ ) symbol to indicate feet, or a (m) to indicate meters.

Paper format and size.

This lesson will use the US standard Letter (8 ½” x 11”) paper size. Feel free to use a paper size that you are familiar with or can easily print from.

The file used in this lesson.

This lesson will open using the training file created from Part 2 of this lesson series. If you would like to continue using your own model, once you have entered this lesson, navigate to the Main Menu (top left icon) , then click Open to find your file. Chapter 1: Organize for Export

In order to be able to print and cut the model, we need to unfold the surfaces. Grouping sections of the model will allow us to move them as pieces. Planning is critical to get the model flattened.

Make a copy

  • Making a copy gives us a chance to experiment and try out different strategies to unfold the model.
  • Using the Select tool, drag a window around the entire model to preselect everything.
  • With the Move tool, click Ctrl [PC] or Option [Mac], then copy the model anywhere in the model space away from the existing model.

Group each facade

  • Think of the model as a packaging box in reverse. We need to isolate each section to determine where we want folds to be.
  • The steps after this will refer to different groups or walls of the model. Use the image to the right to help plan out the grouping process.

Wall 1

  • With the Select tool, Double click on the wall 1 face, hold down shift, and then click on the two windows to add them to the selection.
  • Right-click on the face and select Make Group to isolate Wall 1 from the rest of the model.

Make sure shift is held down while adding to your selection otherwise it will deselect your previously selected objects.

Wall 2

  • With the Select tool, double-click on the wall face, hold down shift, then double-click on the roof above, and single click on the sliding door to add it to the selection.
  • Right-click on the face and select Make Group to isolate Wall 2 from the rest of the model.

Wall 3

  • Repeat the process for Wall 3 as described in the previous grouping actions.
  • Remember to pre-select the roof and wall with a double-click and the three windows and the door with a single click while holding down shift.

Wall 4

  • Repeat the process for Wall 4 as described in the previous grouping actions.
  • Remember to pre-select the wall with a double-click and the window with a single click while holding down shift.

Wall 5

  • Repeat the process for Wall 5 as described in the previous grouping actions.
  • Remember to pre-select the roof and walls with a double-click and the door with a single click while holding down shift.
  • Note that the wall has a break in it. Make sure to select both sections of the wall in your grouping.

Wall 6

  • Repeat the process for Wall 6 as described in the previous grouping actions.
  • Remember to pre-select the roof and wall with a double-click action and the window with a single click while holding down shift.

Chapter 2: Unfold the Roof

In order to print the building, we need to align the roof plane to be parallel with the wall surface. By reversing the fold or angle in it, we are then in a way, unfolding it.

Edit Wall 2’s Group

  • With the Select tool, double-click on the group of wall 2 to open it. You will know that you’re inside the group when the rest of your model appears faded and the group is inside a bounding box.

Activate the Rotate tool

  • Within the group, using the Select tool, double-click on the roof surface to select its face and edges.
  • Activate the Rotate tool.
  • To set the rotation point, place the cursor so it is at the intersection where the wall meets the roof.
  • Notice the axis and angle the Rotate tool is trying to set to. In order to make it easier, select the [Left Arrow] key to lock to the green axis then click the bottom roof corner to start setting the pivot point of rotation.

Flatten Wall 2’s Group

  • Move the cursor along the edge of the roof to set the position to rotate from. Simply click anywhere along the edge of the roof.
  • Start the rotation. Move the cursor to the right and notice the roof now folding back. The existing roof was modeled at 30 degrees. In order to flatten it back to the same angle as the wall, we need to do some math. The wall is at 90 degrees so 90-30=60.
  • Type 60 and press enter. The roof will now be flush with the wall. If your roof is modeled at a different angle make sure you rotate the amount appropriate for your model.

Use a Guide

  • If doing the match for the angle is a challenge, try using a guide to assist.
  • With the Tape Measure tool, double-click on the vertical edge of the wall. This creates a guide along the edge and can aid in snapping to the wall edge. It can also serve as a way to verify afterward that the roof is parallel to the wall.

Flatten Wall 3’s Group

  • Repeat the same process and steps similar to the previous group rotation. Use the Right Arrow key to align the rotation plane properly.

Flatten Wall 5’s Group

  • Repeat the same process and steps similar to the previous group with a roof in it. Use the Right Arrow key to align the rotation plane properly.

Flatten Wall 6’s Group

  • Repeat the same process and steps similar to the previous group with a roof in it. Use the Left Arrow key to align the rotation plane on the green axis.

Cleanup inside the box

  • The Follow Me tool created extra geometry when creating the roof pitch in lesson 2.
  • These faces are unnecessary and should be deleted to keep the model clean.
  • Use the Select tool and triple-click on the face of the geometry. This selects all the connected edges and faces. Because the rest of the model is grouped, this will not select any of the walls or the floor plan.
  • Press the delete key to remove the geometry.

Chapter 3: Unfold the Groups

Because the building has two sections, in order to print them without walls and roofs overlapping, we need to separate them into two areas of the model and then unfold the groups down and onto the ground.

Separate in 2 models

  • With the Select tool, click holding shift down on the 3 sections that make up the right portion of the house.

Move section 2 over

  • Using the Move tool, click to start the move and move to the right just enough so that when you fold down the walls to the ground, they will not overlap any other groups.

Flatten Wall 2’s Group

  • Similar to rotating the roofs, select the group and activate the Rotate tool.
  • Using the bottom of the wall as a reference point, rotate the object down 90 degrees so it is flat on the ground.

Flatten Wall 1’s Group

  • Repeat the process to the adjacent group.

Flatten Wall 6’s Group

  • Repeat the process similar to the adjacent group.

Flatten the remaining groups

  • Orbit over to the other part of the building.
  • Repeat the process of unfolding and flattening the groups.

Chapter 4: Cleanup the Floor

Now that the walls are on the ground, the footprint of the floor plan can also be separated into 2 sections to assist in constructing a physically printed model.

Add break in the floor

  • Using the Line tool, draw a line from the inside corner to the outside corner of the central part of the house.
  • This breaks the floor surface into two faces.

Erase

  • Using the Eraser tool, remove the 3 edges of the right portion of the house that was moved over.
  • This allows for the roof to not overlap with the floor plan.

Add floor edge to other building section

  • Since we only moved the three wall groups over, no faces or edges are shown to represent where the floor is.
  • Using the Line tool, trace from corner to corner to give the floor area an edge to cut along later when printing.

Chapter 5: Prep for Print

We’re so close! Now that the model is flat, we need to be able to view it in a way that allows us to print or download an image of the model. If we click on a top view, we see the inside of the box. We want to see the outside, so we actually need to print looking upside down.

Yes, I repeat, we need to look upside down. Imagine a cardboard box or package. If you unfold the top and cut or unfold the sides, you are left looking at all of the inside surfaces of the box. We need to then flip the box by orbiting around it. In SketchUp, we will orbit around and align our view to the bottom.

Let’s look upside down and also see how to print these two different ways. The first is 2 separate pages and the second is all on one page after we move the shapes closer together.

Look upside down

  • Activate the Orbit tool and begin to adjust the view so that you are now looking at the bottom of the model.

Our model is in perspective view and when printing it needs to be aligned so that it is flat like a map.

  • With the Select tool, right-click on the face of the floor area and then select Align View.
    This positions the model so that our camera view looks straight on.


Tip

Having the view flat is crucial to creating an accurate print of the model. If the saved views are angled or in perspective, the model will not print correctly.

Click on the Search icon and type bottom. Click on the Bottom View option and SketchUp will automatically align the view to the bottom. 

Pan the model

  • Now that the view is flat, center the model space using the pan and zoom tools only so that you can see both building sections on the screen.
  • Do not Orbit! If you do, then your view will be out of alignment.

Save a scene

  • Getting back to this view can be a challenge. Saving a scene allows SketchUp to remember this exact camera position.
  • Click on the Scene panel on the right side of the screen.
  • Click on the + icon to add a scene.
  • Notice Scene 2 appears as a thumbnail image.
  • After you orbit around, just click back on Scene 2 to get back to the saved view.

Rename the scene

  • In the scene name area, just click to highlight the text. Begin typing a new name for the scene.
  • Type: One Page Print and press Enter.
  • This scene makes it possible to print this view later in this lesson quickly.

Scenes for a 2-page printout

  • Activate the Zoom Window tool and drag the cursor around the first building section.
  • In the Scene panel, add a new scene and name it “2A Print” and press enter.

Scene for the second page

  • Repeat the previous step but with the second building section. Name the scene “2B Print.”

Chapter 6: Create Connection Tabs

When the model is printed out as a paper cutout the walls can be folded back up. However, where two cut edges meet, a tab or extra piece will be needed to give the adjacent wall something to glue onto. This could be done manually and can take some trial and error. However, models tend to be cleaner and more rigid when the tabs are part of the model.

What is a tab?

A tab simply gives us a place to glue or connect the adjacent surface to.

The design of a tab can be very simple, but its placement can be a challenge. Knowing how the model will fold and connect back up is key.

Add a tab

  • Using the Rotated Rectangle tool, click on the edge of the wall to start a rectangle, and move the cursor along the wall edge until you get to the endpoint.
  • Click at the endpoint and begin to move the cursor out along the wall edge.
  • Type 24 and press enter.

A tab does not have to be wide, just enough to fold and place some glue onto when printed.

For this model, 24 inches appears to be a good size in order to fold and glue the tab to the adjacent surface.

Add a lot more tabs

  • Repeat the process for other parts of the model that need tabs and connections.

Tip

When in doubt, you can always manually add a tab when the model is actually printed out. This can be a lesson in itself. If the tabs are difficult to create, move on to the next step and add the tabs while you are folding and cutting later.

Chapter 7: Print

Now with the tabs in place, we are ready to print our model out onto paper. Various paper sizes can be chosen. For this lesson, Letter size (US 8.5inches x 11 inches) will be shown. However, you can use whatever paper size you are able to print onto.

Go back to your scenes

  • If you moved around in the model, using the Scenes panel, click on scene 2A Print to get back into position.

Print Preview

  • Click on the Menu icon in the top left corner of the screen.
  • Scroll down and select Print.

Print to Scale

  • Select the desired paper size (Letter).
  • Click where it says Current View and select Print to Scale. This will ensure that both printouts are at the same scale

Scale

  • Like a map or a floor plan, models can be scaled down to a measurable unit when printed.
  • Set the scale to 1” = 10’ and ensure the model still fits completely in the viewer. If it is clipped off then select a different scale or pan the model by holding down Shift and dragging the model over.
  • Check the box for “Print scale.”

Save to Print

  • Click Print to PDF to save the view as a PDF. This file type can be easily printed from any device.
  • Press OK on the Please Confirm screen.
  • The PDF will download as an Untitled file. Name the file and save it in your Documents where you’ll be able to find it easily.

Print the other part

  • Repeat the process for the scene “2B Print” with the same drawing scale.

Teacher Tip

Paper Weight

Printing to scale can be a challenge. Most printer settings automatically scale the document down to fit within the printer margins.

In order to get the printed model scale to be accurate, ensure “no scaling” or 100% is being used.

Every program or Operating System has different ways of setting print margins to 0 or printing at 100%. If you are unsure, ask your IT or do an internet search for something like “how to print at 100% in [insert application] (Chrome, Preview, Acrobat, Safari, etc.)

The thicker the paper, the better. Using copy paper is not suggested. Thin paper does not have the same strength as heavier cardstocks. However, some printers may not be able to print on thick paper.

If a thicker paper is not an option, glue additional layers of paper behind the printed model to give it more strength and rigidity.

Chapter 8: Assemble

Now with the tabs in place, we are ready to print our model out onto paper. Various paper sizes can be chosen. For this lesson, Letter size (US 8.5inches x 11 inches) will be shown. However, you can use whatever paper size you are able to print onto.

Careful cutting

  • Using scissors, cut out the perimeter of the building, including the folding tabs.

Fold and fold and fold

  • Fold the smaller tabs first, and then fold along the walls and roof angles.

Getting perfect corner folds

  • Practice and patience are key when you start folding and putting the model together.
  • Using your fingers to crease the edge can be problematic. Try to use a ruler, the end of a pen, or a similar flat object to aid in creasing.

Gluing or Taping

  • Work your way around the corner of the walls to begin putting together the model.
  • Glue is preferred and more forgiving than tape. However, tape can be used as a quick trial to experiment with and doesn’t have to dry as glue does.

Keep going

  • Take your time putting it together, it is not a race!
  • Keep going corner to corner around the model as you begin to roughly assemble the parts.
  • Wait to glue the roof segments until you have assembled the walls.

Angled tabs

  • When two tabs meet an angle that is not 90 degrees, they can produce an overlap.
  • Using scissors, simply create a miter or angled cut so the tab doesn't overlap another tab.

Putting it together

  • With all the walls and tabs folded and positioned, carefully begin to glue the sections together.
  • If necessary, cut open the floor to be able to glue or attach from inside the model.

Bonus Experiment: Trial and Error

Did something go wrong when you tried to put the model together? Do you think a better way of assembling and joining could have been done? Yes and Yes!

A fun exercise with this model is experimenting with better ways to connect the edges. For example, splitting the roof and trying to join it at the top makes for a brittle and unstable top. What if we moved the pieces around to see if it resulted in a sturdier model?

Save as

  • Click the Menu icon in the top right corner of the screen and select Save as to make a copy of the model.

Explode the groups

In order to move the roof surfaces, the groups they are in will need to be separated so we can move just the roof and not the entire group. This is done using the Explode function.

  • With the Select tool, click on the groups using Shift to add to your selection, and then right-click on the groups and select Explode.

Regroup the roofs and walls

  • Using the Select tool, double-click on the 1st roof surface, then right-click and select Make Group.
  • Repeat the process for all the other surfaces using the image in the next step as a reference.

Regroup the walls

  • Visualize where you want new folds and tabs to be and how this might affect the assembly of the house when it’s printed.

Version 2: Roof priority

  • Move and rotate the roof and wall surfaces to configure the model similar to the image to the right.
  • This makes folds at the roof line instead of tabs to explore its strength when printed.

Version 3: Gable wall priority

  • Try moving the items in a way so that everything is folded from the ends of the house.

Version 4: Hybrid

  • Try having a mixture of roof connections and minimal amounts of tabs.
